Student Mental Health in Malaysia 2020

International Islamic University of Malaysia

Malaysia is one of the "leading countries for higher education" in Asia (Kotera, Ting, & Neary, 2021). This could mean increased pressure on students due to the standards of the higher education. Analyzing the mental health epidemic among Malaysian students involves examining a data set obtained from a survey conducted at The International Islamic University of Malaysia (IIUM). IIUM is one of the top public universities in Kuala Lumpur, Malaysia founded in 1983. To get accepted into this this university, students are required to have score of 550 in the Test of English as a Foreign Language (TOEFL) or 6.0 points in International English Language Testing System (IELTS).

Distribution of Depression, Anxiety, and Panic Attacks in IIUM

One hundred students participated in a survey in August 2020 about their mental health, reporting if they have anxiety, depression, and/or panic attacks. 35 students reported having depression, 34 reported having anxiety, and 33 reported having panic attacks from the data provided.

Looking further into the data, there are at least two-thirds of the students who took the survey reported having at least one mental health disorder: depression, anxiety, or panic attacks.

The cumulative grade point average (CGPA) is a mechanism to evaluate a university graduate's knowledge in Malaysia, similar to the GPA system in US college systems. Here, CGPA is measured on a 4.00 scale as well, with higher CGPA meaning a higher level of academic achievement and proficiency in one's courses.

In Figure 3, CGPA was graphed against the number of people who have a mental health disorder present. The majority of the surveyed college students at IIUM have a CGPA above a 3.00 but also have a mental health disorder present. Looking across all the CGPA groups, there was at least one participant with a mental health disorder present.

Results and Conclusions

The findings from this survey reveal the mental health challenges among students in Mayalsia at the start of the fall semester in 2020, shortly following the start of the pandemic. Two-thirds of all responders reported at least one mental health disorder. One-third of students reported having either depression, anxiety, or panic attacks. After comparing the CPGA of students to number of students with a mental health disorder, it is evident that the majority of students have high CGPA and also have a mental health disorder.

By analyzing the student experience concerning their mental health following the COVID-19 pandemic, valuable insights can be gained into how the pandemic affected the mental health of students, even in another country. This analysis can contribute to a broader understanding of the global implications of the pandemic on student well-being, especially after experiencing entirely remote classes and isolation from traditional support systems and social networks.
